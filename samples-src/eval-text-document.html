Field Notes on the Harborwick Tidal Flats

Draft manuscript — fictional text for demonstration purposes

The flats at Harborwick reveal themselves twice daily, retreating water leaving behind a mirror of silt that holds the sky for an hour before the wind roughens it. Visitors who arrive expecting a beach find instead a working landscape: channels rearranged by every storm, sandbars that migrate seasons at a time, and a horizon that never quite agrees with the map.

Early accounts of the estuary describe a shoreline half a mile inland of its present position. The retreat has been neither steady nor quiet. Records kept by the harbor office note years in which the channel deepened enough to admit timber barges, followed by decades in which nothing larger than a rowing skiff could cross at any tide.

What the records do not capture is the sound of the place. At low water the flats tick and whisper — the percussion of countless small lives adjusting to exposure. Wading birds work the margins in unhurried lines, and the light, filtered through coastal haze, flattens distance so completely that walkers routinely misjudge the far bank by half.

This manuscript gathers observations made across four consecutive winters. It makes no claim to completeness. The flats resist completeness; that is, in the end, the argument of the chapters that follow.
